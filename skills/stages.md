# The Six Stages of Design Immersion

SteamHead's Design Immersion cycle adapts the Stanford d.school design thinking
methodology (Empathize → Define → Ideate → Prototype → Test) for elementary
makerspace classes, with one addition: **Inspire**, which is not part of the
d.school cycle. The result is a circle — projects begin when students get
inspired by other people's work, and end when students present their own work
to inspire the next makers.

Note for designers: although the *concept* is a circle, the class skill chart
displays the stages as **columns**. Circular charts proved confusing for young
students tracking their progress.

Skills are placed in a stage by their **focus and use**, not by where an
activity could theoretically appear — many activities (roleplay, sketching,
research) could serve several stages. Each skill's summary states the focus
that justifies its placement. When a placement is argued about, this document
is the reference.

## Inspire (Heart)

The bookend stage, and the only **two-sided** one. Inbound: students get
inspired — they do workshops just to learn, attend maker faires and other
classes' presentations, and consume media about topics and makers. Outbound:
students inspire others — they teach workshops, install their projects in the
real world, and publish their stories. Every Inspire badge (Workshop, Service,
Digital Storytelling) names both sides; the depth ladders currently cover the
outbound side.

## Empathize (Heart)

Understand the people you are designing for: who they are, what they do, why,
and in what environment. Interviews, field research, background research, and
mood boards live here. The output is understanding, not yet a problem
statement.

## Define (Head)

Turn understanding into the problem worth solving. User stories capture
problems as narratives; roleplay acts them out to uncover and define them;
mind maps connect people, places, and problems to find the one that matters;
the driving question states it as a "How might we..." that guides the whole
project. The output is a clear, chosen problem.

## Ideate (Head)

Generate and express many possible solutions — from wild to practical.
Brainstorms produce quantity; sketches and storyboards give ideas enough form
to discuss and compare. Judgment is deferred; the output is options.

## Prototype (Hand)

Plan and build. Work plans and setup-and-cleanup make the building possible;
the Physical Prototype and Digital Prototype chart slots expand into the
Fabrication Tools and Digital Tools wings, where the tool skills live.
Prototypes show ideas in real form — they don't have to work perfectly.

## Test (Hand)

Learn from what you built. Gather feedback from others, beta test with real
users in real environments, and reflect on what worked, what didn't, and what
to do differently. The output feeds the next loop — and the best results flow
back out through Inspire.
