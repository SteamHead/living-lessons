# The Seven Stages of Design Immersion

SteamHead's Design Immersion cycle adapts the Stanford d.school design thinking
methodology (Empathize → Define → Ideate → Prototype → Test) for elementary
makerspace classes, with the cycle opened into a circle by two bookend stages:
**Get Inspired** at the start and **Share** at the end. Projects begin when
students are inspired by other people's work, and end when students present
their own work to inspire the next makers. Both bookends are **Heart** stages —
projects begin and end with Heart, and the Head and Hand work lives in between.

Note for designers: although the *concept* is a circle, the class skill chart
displays the stages as **columns**, left to right in cycle order. Circular
charts proved confusing for young students tracking their progress.

Skills are placed in a stage by their **focus and use**, not by where an
activity could theoretically appear. Each skill's summary states the focus that
justifies its placement. When a placement is argued about, this document is the
reference.

## Get Inspired (Heart)

The opening bookend: students take in other people's work. **Media Explorer**
(read, watch, and listen to maker media), **Learning Station** (do an activity
just to learn it, even if it never joins your project), and **Field Trip**
(attend an event or exhibition as an audience member). These experiences are
often teacher-orchestrated at the lower levels and reach outside school at the
top. Each Get Inspired badge is paired with a Share badge (its outbound twin).

## Empathize (Heart)

Understand the people you are designing for: who they are, what they do, why,
and in what environment. Interviews, field research, background research
(Empowered Researcher), and mood boards live here. The output is understanding,
not yet a problem statement.

## Define (Head)

Turn understanding into the problem worth solving. User stories capture problems
as narratives; roleplay acts them out to uncover and define them; mind maps
connect people, places, and problems to find the one that matters; and the
driving question states it as a "How might we..." that guides the whole project.
The output is a clear, chosen problem.

## Ideate (Head)

Generate and express many possible solutions — from wild to practical.
Brainstorms produce quantity; sketches and storyboards give ideas enough form to
discuss and compare. Judgment is deferred; the output is options.

## Prototype (Hand)

Plan and build. Work plans and setup-and-cleanup make the building possible; the
Physical Prototype and Digital Prototype chart slots expand into the Fabrication
Tools and Digital Tools wings, where the tool skills live. Prototypes show ideas
in real form — they don't have to work perfectly.

## Test (Hand)

Learn from what you built. Gather feedback from others, beta test with real users
in real environments, and reflect on what worked, what didn't, and what to do
differently. The output feeds the next loop.

## Share (Heart)

The closing bookend: students put their work into the world to inspire others.
**Digital Storytelling** (publish the project's story), **Workshop** (teach how
to build it), and **Service** (install it in a real-world location). Each Share
badge is the outbound twin of a Get Inspired badge — the circle closing, as one
class's Share becomes another maker's inspiration.
