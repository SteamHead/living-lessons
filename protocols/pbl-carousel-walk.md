## In a sentence

Projects stay put at stations while makers rotate in small groups to see each
one, leaving a bit of feedback at every stop.

## Why use it

Every project gets seen by everyone, and every maker both presents and
audiences. Better than whole-class presentations when attention is short or the
class is large — nobody waits through twenty turns, and quiet makers share to a
small group instead of the whole room.

## Steps

1. Each maker (or team) sets their work at a station with their Maker's
   Statement or a card beside it.
2. Split the class into small groups, one group per station to start.
3. At each station, the host gives a 30-second show; visitors ask one question
   or leave one kind note.
4. Ring a signal; groups rotate to the next station. The host role travels with
   the station, so a group member may host if the maker rotates.
5. Continue until groups return to their own station and read the notes left
   for them.

## Teacher moves

- Model one good question and one kind note before the first rotation.
- Keep rotations short and signal clearly — the pace is the point.
- Narrate what you see makers doing well ("I heard a question about how it
  works, not just whether it's cool").

## Make it smaller / bigger

*Smaller:* half the class hosts while half walks, then swap — fewer rotations,
easier for K-1. *Bigger:* visitors leave written feedback on sticky notes and
hosts respond to one piece of feedback aloud when groups return.
