## Wonder Question

If you could fix one small problem for someone you know, what would it be?

## Hook

Introduce this run's clients — real people from outside our classroom. Some
years it's a librarian whose bookmarks vanish, a coach whose cones crack, a
grandparent whose remote hides in the couch. The teacher brings the client
list; sometimes YOUR client is someone at home. Either way: a real person,
a real problem, and a maker (you) on the case. This is the whole design
cycle in one project — the one everything else practiced for.

## Mini-Lesson

One stage per session, short and live:

- *Session 1 — Empathize & Define:* who is your client, and what exactly goes
  wrong for them? Draw the problem: who, where, when it happens, how it makes
  them feel. Then brainstorm at least three different solutions — wild ideas
  welcome, labels on everything.
- *Session 2 — Plan & Build:* circle your best solution, sketch it with
  labeled parts and a materials list, get the materials approved, build.
- *Session 3 — Finish & Share:* finish the build, write your statement (one
  sentence each: the problem, what your solution does, who it is for), and
  present at the mini-exhibition.

## The Challenge

**Your goal:** Design and build a solution to your client's problem, and
present it at our mini-exhibition.

**Teacher goal:** See how you keep your client — not yourself — at the center
of every choice, and how your skills from earlier hexagons show up without
being asked for.

**Requirements:**
- The problem drawn and described: who, where, when, and how it feels
- At least three brainstormed solutions before you choose one
- A labeled sketch with a materials list, approved before building
- The build addresses the client's actual problem (useful), and is something
  they'd be glad to receive (beautiful)
- A written statement: problem, solution, who it's for
- Presented at the mini-exhibition; leave everything better than you found it

**Constraints:**
- Materials from the materials table only, within the posted item limit
- Base material (paper or cardboard) is your teacher's call for your class
- Tool licenses apply where cutters and hot glue are used

## Floor & Ceiling

*Floor:* one drawn problem, two brainstormed ideas, and a simple build that
does one useful thing for the client is a complete journey through every
stage. *Ceiling:* interview your client (or the teacher role-playing them)
before designing, test the build against the real problem, or make the
statement persuasive enough to convince a stranger at the exhibition.

## Stretch Goal

Finished early? Prepare your exhibition space like a designer: a title card,
your statement displayed, and the build staged so its purpose is obvious
without you explaining it.

## Badge Evidence

- *User Stories L2:* the drawn problem includes who it happens to, when it
  happens, and how it makes the client feel — simple descriptions added to
  the picture.
- *Brainstorm L2:* three or more labeled solution drawings, including at
  least one wild, non-traditional idea.
- *Sketching L2:* the chosen solution is drawn with labeled parts and a
  materials list a teammate could understand without the maker talking.

## Reflection Menu

- **Tier 1:** Thumbs — would your client actually use this? Raise your hand
  if your final build changed from your first idea.
- **Tier 2:** gallery-walk — at each build, guess the client's problem before
  reading the statement. Did the build explain itself?
- **Tier 3:** Photo of your build and statement to Google Classroom, plus one
  sentence: what did your client's problem teach you that your own ideas
  couldn't?

## Re-entry Prompt

- *Session 2:* Look at your problem drawing from last time. Say your client's
  problem out loud to a partner before touching materials — does your chosen
  solution still fit it?
- *Session 3:* Pick up your build and find the one thing that would embarrass
  you at the exhibition. Fix that first.
