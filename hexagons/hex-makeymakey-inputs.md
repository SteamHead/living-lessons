## Wonder Question

What are three things in your house, besides computers and phones, that have a
computer chip inside?

## Hook

Live demo with one volunteer: complete the circuit through a human touch chain
and the computer responds. Hand out the metal "fruits and vegetables." The
challenge today isn't making it work — it's making it work *without me*.

## Mini-Lesson

Two minutes only: where the instructions live (printed cards +
makeymakey.com), and the fifth-grade frame — following technical instructions
independently is the skill middle school assumes you have. Everything else is
discovered.

## The Challenge

**Your goal:** Using the instructions — not the teacher — make the MakeyMakey
control the computer with at least two different conductive materials. Then
invent one input nobody else at your table tried.

**Teacher goal:** See how you use instructions before asking for help, how you
troubleshoot when a connection fails, and how you help others EVEN if they're
not your friend.

**Requirements:**
- Two working inputs from different materials (foil, body, furniture, objects)
- You can explain *why* your material works (it closes the circuit)
- Photo or video evidence uploaded to Google Classroom
- Kits fully reassembled — count clips before returning

**Constraints:**
- Teacher answers questions only after you've checked instructions AND asked a peer
- Materials from the caddy only

## Floor & Ceiling

*Floor:* the printed "banana piano" card is a guaranteed first success —
follow it exactly and it works. *Ceiling:* design a playable controller layout
for a game (paper + copper tape or clip arrays), or take on a chain challenge
— how many people can one circuit pass through? Ceiling work feeds directly
into Imagination Fair controllers.

## Badge Evidence

- *Learning Station L3:* student follows the station's instructions
  independently to first success (peer help allowed, teacher intervention
  not), finishes the activity, and leaves the station better than found.
- *Craft Circuits L3:* student completes the guided MakeyMakey project, then
  experiments freely with new conductive inputs.

## Reflection Menu

- **Tier 1:** Thumbs — did the instructions get you further than you expected?
  Raise your hand if a classmate got you unstuck.
- **Tier 2:** Table round: each student shows their weirdest working input and
  names one thing that failed first. Teacher check-in: anyone quietly stuck?
  (High-performers can look fine right up until frustration wins — ask.)
- **Tier 3:** Google Classroom upload — video of your working input plus one
  sentence: what did you troubleshoot yourself today?
