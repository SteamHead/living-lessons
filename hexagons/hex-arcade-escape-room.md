## Wonder Question

What makes a locked door exciting instead of annoying?

## Hook

Play one (teacher-picked) escape game together on the projector for five
minutes — get stuck, get unstuck, feel the click. That click is what you're
building: a digital escape room in MakeCode Arcade where the only way out is
through puzzles. And here's the twist that makes yours hard to build and
great to play: the puzzles run on things you're learning this year — but
solved by DOING, not by reading a quiz. Nobody escapes by answering a
multiple-choice question.

## Mini-Lesson

Session by session:

- *Session 1 — Storyboard the escape:* one sticky note per room or puzzle —
  what the player sees, what they must DO, what unlocks. Arrange the notes
  in order, play-test the sequence by talking it through, rearrange until
  the difficulty climbs and the story flows. Puzzle rule: the player acts —
  collects the three fraction keys that sum to one whole, pushes crates onto
  the floor-tiles that make the area 24, walks the conductor path where
  touching an insulator resets you. Words on screen stay short.
- *Session 2 — Build it:* MakeCode Arcade's tilemaps make rooms; walls
  block; overlap events open doors; counters track keys. Start from a
  guided tutorial if you want, then build your storyboarded rooms. Another
  language you already know (Scratch, etc.) is fine if the escape-room goal
  holds.
- *Session 3 — Beta-test & ship:* trade games with another team. Watch them
  play WITHOUT helping — note where they get stuck, surprised, or
  delighted. Make one revision from what you saw. Then it ships.

## The Challenge

**Your goal:** Design and build a playable escape room in MakeCode Arcade
with at least three puzzles a player solves by doing — then beta-test it on
classmates and revise once.

**Teacher goal:** See who storyboards before coding instead of after, who
watches their tester in silence (the hardest skill in this room), and whose
puzzles teach without feeling like a worksheet.

**Requirements:**
- A storyboard on movable panels — one per room/puzzle — arranged and
  rearranged before any code is written
- At least three puzzles solved by player ACTION, each tied to something
  from this year's math or science
- A playable start-to-escape path — you've completed it yourself at least
  twice
- One beta-test observed in silence, notes taken, one revision made from
  the notes
- Text on screen kept minimal — if a puzzle needs a paragraph, redesign
  the puzzle

**Constraints:**
- MakeCode Arcade is the default; another language you know is allowed if
  the project goal holds
- Curriculum topics come from the teacher's list for this year
- No jump-scares; frustration is a design failure, not a difficulty setting

## Floor & Ceiling

*Floor:* one room, one honest doing-puzzle, one working exit — a complete
escape room in miniature, built from the tutorial's bones. *Ceiling:* chain
your puzzles so each unlock changes an earlier room (real escape-room
architecture), or hide an optional secret room for players who notice what
others walk past.

## Stretch Goal

Finished early? Write the hint card a teacher could read aloud for each
puzzle — three hints per puzzle, gentlest first. Good hints are harder to
write than good puzzles: they rescue the player without robbing the click.

## Badge Evidence

- *App Design L3:* the student completes guided work in MakeCode Arcade (or
  a known alternate language), then finishes a design challenge of their
  own — the escape room — as a playable interactive creation.
- *Storyboarding L2:* the escape's story is planned on movable panels —
  sticky notes, one per room/event — physically rearranged until the order
  of events flows.
- *Beta Test L2:* the student watches a classmate play their game without
  helping, and records where the player got stuck, surprised, or delighted.

## Reflection Menu

- **Tier 1:** Raise your hand if your tester got stuck somewhere you never
  expected. Keep it up if watching in silence was harder than coding.
- **Tier 2:** partner-share — tell your beta-tester the one revision their
  play caused; they tell you the moment the game delighted them.
- **Tier 3:** Screenshot of your favorite room to Google Classroom with one
  sentence: which puzzle taught the most, and how do you know a player
  learned it?

## Re-entry Prompt

- *Session 2:* Lay your storyboard panels next to your screen before coding.
  Build in panel order — the storyboard is the boss today.
- *Session 3:* Play your own game once, fresh, before trading. Fix nothing
  yet — just feel where YOU hesitate; your tester will hesitate longer.
