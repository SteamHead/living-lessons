## Wonder Question

What did we learn last time that we can use this time?

## Hook

Photos of round one's towers — the champions and the beautiful collapses.
New twist: this time tall isn't enough. The tower has to be tall AND strong:
it holds 500 grams (a can of beans) for ten seconds.

## Mini-Lesson

Strength and height fight each other. Wide bases resist tipping; rolled tubes
make columns; folds make walls. Ask the question engineers ask: where does the
weight *want* to go?

## The Challenge

**Your goal:** Build the tallest free-standing paper tower that holds 500
grams or more on top.

**Teacher goal:** Carrying over — do teams actually use what round one
taught, and does the five-minute plan change what gets built?

**Requirements:**
- Free-standing: cannot lean, cannot be held
- Holds the weight for ten seconds
- A team plan sketch made before building
- Leave the makerspace better than you found it

**Constraints:**
- Paper only, no tools, no anchoring the base
- Five minutes to plan as a team, then fifteen minutes to build

## Floor & Ceiling

*Floor:* the round-one fold guarantees every team a standing start, and
holding ANY weight counts as data worth talking about. *Ceiling:* predict
before testing — how much will it hold? Then test to failure and explain
where and why it buckled.

## Badge Evidence

- *Card Craft L1:* folds, tubes, and base shapes used as deliberate
  strengthening moves — chosen on purpose this round, not discovered by
  accident.
- *Sketching L1:* the team plan drawing is clear enough that a team member can
  explain the tower while pointing at it.

## Reflection Menu

- **Tier 1:** Raise your hand if your team's plan changed during the build —
  that's normal; someone say why yours changed.
- **Tier 2:** Failure autopsy walk — each team points at exactly where their
  tower buckled and names the force that won.
- **Tier 3:** Photo of the loaded tower to Google Classroom with one sentence:
  what would round three look like?
