## Wonder Question

What lights turned on for you today before you even got to school — and how
did each one know to turn on?

## Hook

Hold up the two parts: a coin battery and an LED. "These can make light
together. I am not going to tell you how. No instructions today — you and
your table are going to figure it out." Hand out the caddies and step back.

## Mini-Lesson

Held until most tables have first light, then on the rug: what did you
discover? Formalize it together — long leg, short leg, direction matters,
metal touches metal. Electricity needs a complete path, and the LED is a
one-way door. Micro-tip for builds later: tape onto tape, not bare paper.

## The Challenge

**Your goal:** Make the LED turn on using only the battery — no instructions,
no teacher recipe. When you solve it, help another maker without doing it for
them.

**Teacher goal:** See who keeps trying after the first flip doesn't work, and
*how* students help — hints and pointing, or taking over.

**Requirements:**
- Your LED lights, and you can say the rule you discovered (which leg, which
  side, what touches what)
- Help at least one other maker with words and pointing — not your hands
- Batteries and LEDs return to the caddy, counted

**Constraints:**
- One battery per student; LEDs from the caddy only
- Tape comes out only after first light

## Floor & Ceiling

*Floor:* flipping the LED until it lights IS the lesson — a wrong guess is
data, and helping hands (words only) are always nearby. *Ceiling, K-2:* light
a second LED, or become a table helper — then carry your lit LED into the
space station build (see hex-space-station-modules). *Ceiling, grades 3-5:*
after discussing what LEDs do in the world, connect two LEDs to one battery
and test which colors light together (red + orange? blue + green?) and which
refuse to share (red + blue?). Record your pairs, then join the rug talk on
forward voltage: different colors need different pushes, and a 3V coin cell
can't satisfy everyone at once.

## Badge Evidence

- *Craft Circuits L1:* student lights an LED directly on a coin cell — long
  leg, short leg, direction matters, metal touches metal — and can show and
  say the rule they discovered, not just repeat a demonstration.

## Reflection Menu

- **Tier 1:** Raise your hand if your first try didn't work. Keep it up if
  you kept going anyway. That's the whole job.
- **Tier 2:** Table round — each maker demonstrates their light and teaches
  the rule in their own words to a partner.
- **Tier 3:** (Grades 3-5) Photo of your two-LED pairs to Google Classroom
  with one sentence: which colors cooperated, and your best guess why.
