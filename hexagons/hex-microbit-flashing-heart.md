## Wonder Question

The screen you're looking at right now is following instructions someone wrote.
What do you think an instruction to a computer actually looks like?

## Hook

Hold up a micro:bit: this is a whole computer, small enough to fit in your
hand, and today YOU write the instructions it follows. Quick tour of the
board — the LED grid that shows pictures, the two buttons, the little chip
that's the brain. Then the promise: by the end, you'll make it flash a heart,
and it'll be *your* code doing it.

## Mini-Lesson

Two minutes only. Where the tools live: makecode.microbit.org on the laptop,
and the Flashing Heart tutorial right on the home page. How code runs: you
snap blocks together like instructions in order, press Download, and move the
program onto the micro:bit over the cable. That's it — the tutorial teaches
the rest, and you follow it, not the teacher.

## The Challenge

**Your goal:** Complete the Flashing Heart tutorial on makecode, get it onto a
micro:bit (or run it in the on-screen simulator), then change it — make it
flash a shape you designed.

**Teacher goal:** See who reads the tutorial before asking, who helps a
neighbor get unstuck, and who keeps going after the first thing doesn't work.

**Requirements:**
- Flashing Heart tutorial completed — running on a micro:bit or the simulator
- You changed something: your own shape, or how fast it flashes
- You can point to the board's parts — LED grid, buttons, brain — and say
  what the buttons and shake can sense
- Laptop and micro:bit returned the way you found them, cables coiled

**Constraints:**
- The tutorial and a peer come before the teacher for help
- One micro:bit per maker where kits allow; share the simulator otherwise

## Floor & Ceiling

*Floor:* the tutorial's built-in step highlights guarantee a flashing heart
even if you follow it click-for-click — that's still real code you ran.
*Ceiling:* design a two-frame animation (heart, then a different shape) so it
looks like it's moving, or figure out how to make it flash faster and slower.

## Badge Evidence

- *Electronic Sensors L2:* student runs a program on a classroom board
  (their own downloaded code, from the tutorial), can point out the board's
  main parts, and names what its built-in inputs — buttons, shake — detect.
  (Running-a-program plus naming-the-parts is the L2 rung; writing original
  input→output logic is L3, which comes in the next hexagon.)

## Reflection Menu

- **Tier 1:** Raise your hand if you made a computer do something for the first
  time today. Keep it up if you helped someone else get theirs to flash.
- **Tier 2:** How did you feel when you first opened the website? How did you
  feel once your design flashed? Say both to a partner.
- **Tier 3:** Photo or short video of your flashing design to Google
  Classroom with one sentence: what did you change from the tutorial?
