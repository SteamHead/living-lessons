## Wonder Question

How do you know when something you built is actually finished?

## Hook

Engineers don't start by building — they start by writing down what "done"
means, and then they're honest about whether they hit it. Today you're the
client AND the engineer: you'll make something for yourself, but first you'll
write the spec you have to live up to, and you'll draw constraint cards that
make it harder on purpose. At the end, nobody grades you against your spec
except you — and you don't get to pretend.

## Mini-Lesson

Two tools, five minutes. **Constraint cards:** draw two from the deck — each
adds a rule your build must obey (a moving part, a weight it must hold, a
size it must fit). The cards are the universe being difficult; welcome to
engineering. **The spec:** before building, write three requirements of your
own — specific enough to check ("holds my three markers upright," not "is
good") — plus your two card constraints. Sketch the design with labeled
parts, what each part does, and materials. That page is your contract with
yourself.

## The Challenge

**Your goal:** Make something for yourself that satisfies your own written
spec AND your two constraint cards — then score it honestly.

**Teacher goal:** See who writes requirements that can actually be checked,
who treats a difficult card as a design puzzle instead of bad luck, and who
scores their own build honestly at the end.

**Requirements:**
- Two constraint cards drawn and kept visible at your workspace
- A written spec before building: three checkable requirements + the two
  card constraints
- A labeled sketch: parts, what each does, materials
- The build, made by you, for you
- The self-check, done at the end: go requirement by requirement and mark
  each MET / PARTLY / MISSED — with one honest sentence about the weakest one
- Space left better than you found it

**Constraints:**
- Whatever your two cards say — those are the real constraints today
- Materials from the table only; tool licenses apply

## Floor & Ceiling

*Floor:* one constraint card instead of two, two requirements instead of
three — the write-build-check loop intact is what matters, not the count.
*Ceiling:* write one requirement with a NUMBER in it (holds 300 grams, fits
a 20 cm shelf) and prove it with a measurement, or trade specs with a
neighbor and each check the other's build cold.

## Stretch Goal

Finished and self-checked? Bring your build and spec to the teacher — they'll
request exactly ONE change, like a real client would. Make the revision and
note on your spec what changed and why. That's version 2, and version 2 is
where engineering actually lives.

## Badge Evidence

- *Sketching L3:* the pre-build page is a technical drawing of the planned
  solution with material labels and short descriptions of what each part
  does — tied to written, checkable requirements.
- *Card Craft L4:* the build uses cardboard, hot glue, and cardboard cutters
  in a custom design of the maker's own, tool safety remembered throughout.

## Reflection Menu

- **Tier 1:** Hold up your self-check. Raise your hand if you marked
  anything MISSED — and keep it up if you wrote the honest sentence anyway.
- **Tier 2:** partner-share — read your weakest requirement aloud and what
  you'd change; the partner answers with what the build does BETTER than the
  spec promised.
- **Tier 3:** Photo of build + spec + self-check to Google Classroom, plus
  one sentence: was your spec too easy, too hard, or just right — and how do
  you know?
